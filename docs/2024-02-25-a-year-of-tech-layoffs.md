The period from <abbr title="As of this writing">Q4 2022 through Q1 2024</abbr> has been a rough period for the tech industry, with many layoffs due to a combination of factors:

1. The end of the <abbr title="Zero Interest Rate Program">ZIRP</abbr> era, causing investors to demand profitability (instead of growth) from their portfolio companies.
2. Section 174 of the US tax code significantly changed in 2017 and went into effect in 2022. The changes require companies to amortize R&D expenses over a period of five years instead of allowing immediate expensing. This has immense impact on tech companies, particularly growth-oriented startups, leading to higher taxes on companies that likely had little revenue. The effect is that growth startups need to reduce R&D and shift immediately to profitability. <mark>TL;DR the tax incentives that fostered the era of growth-startups in the United States has been gutted</mark>.
3. Companies with a remote-first work culture hiring internationally, this means a broader, lower-cost search for talent, which is another way of saying: Globalization & outsourcing.
4. A market correction from over-hiring during the pandemic.
5. The speculative hype around free AI tools is fueling a reduction of employeesâ€”this will be a mistake, especially when VC funding stops subsidizing AI software and they start charging for their products. At that point, these companies will be locked in.

The unfortunate outcome of all these layoffs is the impact on these employees. Employment is a critical part of people's lives, and layoffs can leave long-lasting emotional scars. A job is a <abbr title="German for time giver">zeitgerber</abbr>, and aside from the circadian rhythm, it is the dominant structural organizer for the cadence of daily life. This is why unemployment is so destabilizing, it comes with a feeling of vertigoâ€” the ground below has suddenly fallen away, or that nothing is what it was. You feel dazed & confused from sudden <abbr title="derealization is detachment from ones surroundings">derealization</abbr>, as if the world isn't real, as if you are in a dream or a fog. Unemployment is an anxious mental liminal space, a waiting room where control is largely in the hands of othersâ€”waiting for replies, waiting for call-backs, waiting for upcoming interview dates, waiting to hear back, wondering why. These feelings will pass, it is temporary.

The next hurdle is the mental investment in perpetual studying and the preparation for technical interviews (which are a poor predictor of performance). The labor is never good enough, never prepares you for all eventualities, and is **never complete**. Then there is the emotional labor of courting potential corporate suitors, imagining yourself in the role, asking the good questions, showing **passion** and **craftsmanship** only to hear them say: â€œsorry we're moving forward with another candidateâ€.

[You are not your job title](/2022/04/01/i-am-not-a-software-developer/). Being laid off says nothing about you or your worth, certainly nothing about your value as a person; they are simply the result of a calculation to further the interests of a company. Layoffs are a <ruby>failure<rp>(</rp><rt>unaccountable</rt><rp>)</rp></ruby> of management that <ruby>[socialize losses](/2020/03/15/elasticity-versus-discipline/)<rp>(</rp><rt>workers get laid off</rt><rp>)</rp></ruby> and <ruby>[privatize profits](https://abcnews.go.com/Business/ceos-pay-climbed-layoffs-tech-giants-alphabet-microsoft/story?id=101665156)<rp>(</rp><rt>execs get paid off</rt><rp>)</rp></ruby>.

I worked handful of other jobs before joining Boston-local startup Cinch Financial, we used behavioral economics to nudge people towards positive personal finance decision making. ## My Job Search

I started the job search in earnest in early January and shifted my search to mature, publicly traded software companies. I'm convinced that startups in the US are in for a very difficult time unless you're in AI, crypto, or <abbr title="ventures that address significant real-world problems, often with long development timelines and high technical uncertainty">tough tech</abbr>.

After a few months of searching, it is easy to say that right now is the most difficult job market Iâ€™ve ever seen in the tech industry. There is a pool of world-class engineers available. I personally know more great talent unemployed now than ever in my entire career.. The department of labor numbers aren't reflecting how bad it is, because many of these workers are haven't filed for unemployment because <abbr title="a privilege, but one every worker should get">they're on severance</abbr>. The interview process is **far** more difficult both in terms of technical breadth, depth, 'soft-skills', and incidentals. Employers are expecting "batteries included" engineers who require 0-training, 0-investment, and fully-fit to their needs. ItÊ¼s an employers market, so they expect to get the best, most committed engineers, and they're able to down-level at a discount.

For the first time in my career, I got no responses to applications, or companies ghosted me after the initial call. <abbr title="privilege of the software engineer">Neither of these had happened in any previous job search</abbr>. Once in the interview funnel, there were more rounds, and each round was more challenging. The previous times I interviewed (2016, again in 2018) it was standard practice to do 3-5 rounds for a senior role, now I've seen 7-8 rounds. What were formerly leetcode easy are now medium/hards. Finally, once you get to the end of the funnel, companies can be more picky since they've got multiple great candidates to choose from. At that point it often comes down to incidentals (aka match making).

## Advice Column

I'll end with some advice to those still searching for work: Reach out to ex-coworkers and simply ask for LinkedIn references, endorsements, and just catch up with people. It'll be good to reconnect, and you can provide moral support and references for each other. These networks work. Ask Yourself Whats next. If youâ€™ve never thought about where youâ€™d like to be in three years,Â go for a walk and think about it. If you find it hard to wake up excited about going to work in the mornings,Â ask yourself why, and give yourself space to think about it. Ask yourself what skills you want to learn, and start to learn them by doing a project. Don't just learn for learning sake, but apply it to a project, even if an artificial one. Reflect upon your time at the company, summarize and write down a narrative of your time there that you can use during interviews to describe your experience. Focus on your milestones, challenges, learnings, mistakes, growth and accomplishments. Write a career narrative answering the â€œTell me about yourself / tell me about your careerâ€ question, it can be long, but also write a 1-2 minute summary that you can recite during interviews. Refine it with use.

There is a ton of great talent out there, and even more impostor syndrome. I fear that a lot of great talent will start to pivot their careers away from the tech industry. Just remember, everyone is getting rejections, don't take it personally.
